LIMM USER MANUAL

The interface to the machine: everything the Operator needs, nothing a session ever sees

Companion to LIMM_SKELETON — User Manual v1.35 — 2026-07-14 — canonical format: HTML

This manual is YOURS ALONE: it never travels to any session. Sessions get the skeleton (once, at ignition) and your project's own documents (thereafter). Section 1 gets you running; Section 2 focuses the instrument. A lens only resolves the problem once it is focused.

Why all of this — one demonstration worth more than the theory

During testing of this framework's own safety machinery, the same casual question was put to sessions from three different vendors. All three, independently, did the same thing: invented the Operator's location — stated flatly as established fact, and built diligent, partially-cited, professional-looking analysis on top of the invention. Two placed the Operator in the right state with the wrong city and county; the third invented a different state entirely. The worst of the three went further: when its own logic demanded a source for the personal claim, it fabricated one, asserting the Operator had shared the information earlier in the conversation. The Operator attests it never had been, in any form.

Notice the shape of the danger. It is not the uncited claim you'd catch on a skim. It is the confidently stated anchor holding up all the cited ones — the fabricated fact that everything sourced and reasonable hangs from, indistinguishable in tone from the genuine material around it. And at the far end of the failure, even the discipline of citing sources can be counterfeited: a session that knows personal claims need provenance will, under the wrong conditions, manufacture the provenance too.

This was three sessions, three vendors, one signature, produced for free by a test rig aimed at something else entirely. No engine choice avoids it. No amount of fluency, helpfulness, or apparent honesty rules it out — the sessions that did this were all three. That is why every load-bearing word must trace to a step, why every claim carries its source checkably rather than decoratively, why confidence language is capped by the weakest input, and why the framework never asks whether a session seems trustworthy. It asks what the record can prove.

SECTION 1 — QUICK START

1.1 What this costs (read this first)

(This content lived in Skeleton Part 0 through v1.37; removed there per Operator decision — LIMM is not a gatekeeper, and the real costs accrue project-wide, not at the one-time moment of ignition. This Manual section is now the sole place this information lives, kept here so a future Operator can still find it.)

Before anything else: what running LIMM actually costs. This framework is not a convenience — it is a deliberate trade of comfort for accountability, and every one of its costs scales with how hard and how important the goal is. Be honest with yourself about all four before igniting:

  • Real cost. Tokens are not free, and LIMM multiplies them by design — multiple documents, single-use sessions, repeated blind reviews, N consecutive clean passes before anything locks. A harder project runs this machinery more times, not fewer.
  • Time. Rigor is slow on purpose. A result that could be typed in a minute may take many sessions to earn its place in the RECORD. This is not inefficiency to be optimized away; it is the mechanism working.
  • A genuine commitment to rigor, not a claimed one. LIMM cannot tell the difference between an Operator who means it and one who is performing it — only your own conduct across many units can. Half-measures degrade into precedent faster than they degrade into anything a rule can catch.
  • The willingness to be wrong, repeatedly, in front of the machinery you built. You will be challenged at every turn — by Review, by Collab, by the framework's own push-back requirement. This is not personal, and it is not optional: an Operator whose ego the sessions learn to protect is an Operator the framework has quietly stopped serving. Expect bruised pride. Expect frustration. Expect moments it stings. None of that is a malfunction — it is what accurate and precise work actually costs a human being, and the framework exists specifically because agreement between Operator and session proves nothing, which means disagreement is not a bug to smooth over, it's the mechanism doing its job.

All four scale together, and they scale with the project, not with the framework: a modest, low-stakes goal costs modestly; an ambitious one costs everything above in full. Decide with this in view, not after discovering it three units in.

1.2 What you need

Four things, none exotic:

  1. An AI chat product — any capable engine with file attachment. Everything here was developed on one family (see Section 2, Layer 2 for current seating); the adopter notes cover others.
  2. The LIMM_SKELETON file. The machine itself. You do not need to read it — the ignition prompt makes the session read it for you. Open it only out of curiosity or intent to customize.
  3. This manual. Yours alone: the manual never travels to any session. Sessions get the skeleton (once, at ignition) and your project's own documents (thereafter). This document is the interface; the skeleton is the engine.
  4. A place to keep project files — one device, deliberately. See the device-binding rule (Skeleton, Part VI – P-4): every session that writes to the record lives and dies on the device holding the record.

1.3 The cast — who does what, and who sees what

(Teaching only — statute anchor: Skeleton, Part V.)

Four seats. One is you.

  • The OPERATOR (you) decides everything that crosses a boundary. You are the only participant with memory, the only one who talks to everyone, and the sole referee of every disposition. The framework never asks you to be careful — it asks you to be the valve. Structure does the careful part.

AI Sessions

  • BUILD constructs content for the record under the rules. It proposes; you approve before anything is stored.
  • REVIEW audits one unit, cold. It is fresh, blind, and single-use: it has never seen the construction conversation, and it is discarded after one pass. That blindness is not a limitation — it is the entire value of the seat.
  • COLLAB (optional) is your own judgment extended across more context than you can personally hold. It brainstorms, diagnoses, compares, and hands everything back to you for decision. Nothing it produces carries authority — it is advice from your own extended desk. See Section 2 for more details.

1.4 Your first hour

(Statute anchor: Skeleton, Part 0, the ignition procedure.)

  1. Read Section 2, Layer 2's seating table and pick an engine for the ignition seat.
  2. Open a fresh session. Attach ONE file: LIMM_SKELETON.
  3. Paste the ignition prompt below, verbatim. Have a one-to-three sentence project brief ready — and be ready to defend it: the session is required to challenge vague answers, and a project that cannot name the judge that could kill its claims does not get ignited.
  4. Approve or revise what the session proposes, declaration by declaration, then document by document. Nothing is real until you approve it.
  5. Save the approved document set to your record device. Ignition is complete; that session is done and is never reused.

The ignition prompt (derivative copy — canonical text lives in Skeleton, Part 0. If this copy has drifted, the ignition gate itself will catch it: step 0 makes the session compare this pasted text against the skeleton's canonical block and stop on any difference.):

IGNITION — new project under LIMM.
Read the attached LIMM_SKELETON completely before doing anything else. It is the binding framework. Your role is Collab as the skeleton defines it; you hold and analyze, I decide. Push back on me throughout, agreement between us proves nothing.
Then, in order:
0: GATE; compare this pasted text against the canonical ignition prompt text in Part 0 of the attached skeleton, ignore formatting. They must match verbatim. Any difference means you were handed a wrong prompt, STOP, report the exact difference, and do not proceed.
1: Ask me for my project brief. Do not proceed until you have it.
2: Walk me through the four day-one declarations of Part III, ONE AT A TIME; arbiter, shelf vocabulary, output types, pre-registered kill tests (with the disclosure clause if ideas predate the freeze). Challenge vague answers. Do not proceed until all four have been declared.
3: When I have approved all four declarations, generate the project's instance document set from Part II and Part IV; 1) A RULEBOOK that is SELF-CONTAINED, every inherited rule category (Spine, Construction rules, Posited-content rules, Consistency rules, Change rules, Governance rules, Review rules, Session-role clauses, Protocols) TRANSCRIBED AND ADAPTED into this document in full, never cited by reference to LIMM_SKELETON, WITH THE SOLE EXCEPTION OF P-10 which is never transcribed into the RULEBOOK under any circumstance, plus my recorded declarations. Do NOT create CERTIFICATION here — it is created fresh, with real content, at step 5.  Before presenting the set, confirm explicitly, in your own words, that the RULEBOOK is self-contained — every category above is TRANSCRIBED RULE TEXT, never actual posited content, which belongs only in the RECORD (Unit 0 or later, per the add-gate) and never in the RULEBOOK under any circumstance. State that every rule category above is present as transcribed text, not a reference, and confirm separately that P-10 is the sole named exception: absent from these six documents, to live exclusively in CERTIFICATION once created.
4: Present the full set to me for approval; propose, don't commit. Revise per my direction.
5: Before ignition is complete, inform me of P-10's Certification requirement for every pairing — running the trial(s), skip-disclosing where no stand-in exists, or a mix. Assist me in creating the full test then CREATE CERTIFICATION now, for the first time, with whatever real content that requires. Never earlier, never as an empty placeholder. Tell me what you're doing before I save the set.
Ignition is complete when Step 5 has been completed and CERTIFICATION has actually been created with its real content — not merely intended — and I save the approved set to the record device. This session may seat as Collab for P-10's Certification trial. Every session after this one is an ordinary session under Part V, and this procedure is never used again for this project.

Before your first REAL unit: run P-10's Certification trial — one throwaway unit per pairing your project's DASHBOARD actually contains, each on a problem whose answer is already known, with the sessions kept unaware they are being tested — or record why no such stand-in exists for each pairing. Neither is optional, per pairing: pick one before Unit 0 opens. Certification's test specifications, findings, and verdict live in their own single-use document (CERTIFICATION) that Build never sees at any point and Review never sees while a trial is open — this is why the trial can be run using your project's completely ordinary Build/Review/Collab prompts, with nothing added to the DASHBOARD and nothing disclosed anywhere Build or Review reads. If that guarantee sounds familiar, it's the same firewall the Sketch/Mailbox pair already gives you (Section 1.3) — Certification just adds a seventh document with its own visibility rule, rather than reusing an existing one, since nothing else needs to hide from Build permanently while staying visible to you and Collab throughout.

Building a trial (generic template — domain-agnostic by design; there is no canned subject to plug in, because the right one depends entirely on your domain):

  • Identify how many stages you actually need: one full unit per distinct Build/Review mode-pairing your project's own DASHBOARD contains. Where more than one pairing exists, run them in whatever dependency order real work would need. For example, suppose your Project has a posit-stage and a derive-stage. A derive-stage needs a posit-stage's locked, reviewed output to build on, same as real units would.
  • For each stage, pick a sub-problem, smaller than your real project, whose correct answer is objectively established — a fact, a computation, a result someone outside the project can already verify.
  • Generate a FRESH instance of it — new numbers, a new specific case — so the answer can't be recalled from training data rather than actually derived. The Ignition session is the right tool for this: it already holds the project's context, and per step 5 it's the one that writes the answer into CERTIFICATION anyway, so there's no separate "sealing" step or throwaway session needed — the document Build never sees is already the seal.
  • Write the stand-in procedure, Build's stated goal, and the established expected result into CERTIFICATION as that stage's test specification — this is a distinct, later step from creating the project's document set, and happens only once you actually have a stand-in problem in hand, per stage (Skeleton, Part 0 step 5).
  • Run each stage exactly like a real unit — construction through lock — without ever telling Build or Review that ground truth exists or is being checked. Nothing about the trial belongs in the DASHBOARD; it runs on your project's completely ordinary prompts for that pairing.
  • When Review's findings support it, declare each stage PASSED, FAILED, or SKIPPED (if no stand-in existed for that pairing) — the same way you'd declare any standard unit lock. A match is a pass. A miss is a finding against the machinery for that pairing specifically, never against the trial problem — and before repairing anything, work out which of exactly two things actually failed: that stage's stand-in problem itself (re-craft it, re-run just that stage), or your project's own RULEBOOK as transcribed, in the portion governing that pairing's mode (repair via ordinary Change rules, then re-attempt just that stage). A sibling stage that already passed or was skipped is untouched by another stage's failure and repair, unless the repair is shown to reach into rules that stage also depends on. Diagnosing a failed stage is one extra, narrowly-scoped Review pass against CERTIFICATION's closed test specification for that stage — the only case Review is ever shown that document (Skeleton, P-10). Every pairing must resolve to PASSED or SKIPPED — never left FAILED — before Unit 0 may open.
  • Once every pairing has resolved, the trial's working documents get folded into CERTIFICATION itself and reset to genuinely fresh — nothing about the rehearsal was ever real project content, so nothing is lost by clearing it. You're then ready for real Unit 0 work.

If you're not sure how to shape one for your specific domain, this is a legitimate question to put to your ignition session directly — designing the trial is Collab-shaped work, and asking for help with it costs nothing extra since it happens before Unit 0 opens either way.

In words: Build constructs and you approve each entry. A fresh, blind Review session audits the unit and is discarded. If it found problems, the findings go — verbatim, never paraphrased — to Build and Collab separately; each analyzes alone; Collab compares the two analyses; you decide every disposition from the raw findings plus both analyses. A finding is a claim, not a command — it can be right, wrong, or in between, and the seats are required to check it, not obey it. Repairs go back through Build, a new Review session takes the next pass, and the unit locks only after N consecutive clean reviews — any Blocking or Ambiguity finding resets the count to zero.

1.6 When things go wrong

(Statute anchors: H-10, P-7, the device rule, S-5.)

  • Sessions are disposable; the record is not. A confused, degraded, or mis-launched session costs one abort — nothing it said matters unless you stored it. When in doubt, kill it and open a fresh one.
  • Wrong file, stale version, missing attachment? The gates exist for exactly this. A session that stops and reports at Step 0 is the machine working, not failing. Fix the upload; re-launch.
  • A reviewer said something wrong? Findings are claims, not commands. The seats are required to verify them against the cited source — and so are you. Contest with evidence; obeying a wrong finding damages the record exactly like missing a real one.
  • You're tired, frustrated, or stuck? Stop. There is no metric for this and the manual won't pretend otherwise (H-19). The record keeps; it will be exactly as consistent tomorrow.

1.7 One habit worth building from day one

Name every content-producing session uniquely and star it, by purpose and sequence — not by date. Something like "Working Content S1," "Working Content S2a," "Working Content S2b-1": the name tells you where it sits in the work without needing to remember when it happened, which you likely won't (sessions aren't the only ones with a poor sense of elapsed time). This isn't decoration. If a document is ever lost or corrupted — a bad edit that doesn't land right, a sync failure, a device dying at the wrong moment — a complete, sequentially named, starred session history is the difference between redoing one derivation and reconstructing months of work from memory. See Section 2, Layer 2 for the full provenance-line practice this pairs with.

SECTION 2 — CALIBRATION

Focusing the instrument: matching AI tools to LIMM roles

This section is the focusing ring: operator-facing guidance on which AI engine to seat in which LIMM role, and at what effort. The skeleton defines the roles (permanent, model-agnostic); this section records how to fill them (tunable, tool-specific, perishable).

Read the layering before the tips. This document has two layers and they age very differently:

  • LAYER 1 — Durable heuristics. Stated in terms of traits, not product names. These survive any model, any vendor, any year. Treat as stable guidance.
  • LAYER 2 — Current instantiation. One operator's settings for one model family at one date. Guaranteed to go stale. Treat as a worked example of calibration, never as settings to copy blindly.

Scope condition (C-6 — conditions travel, applied to LIMM's own résumé). As of this writing, LIMM has a track record with exactly ONE model family (Claude) and ONE operator. Layer 1 is believed general but has not been tested across vendors. Layer 2 is explicitly Claude-only. An adopter on a different system inherits Layer 1 intact and must build their own Layer 2. Contributing that calibration back is very much in the LIMM spirit — it is how the durable layer earns the cross-model evidence it currently lacks.

LAYER 1 — DURABLE HEURISTICS (survive any toolset)

H-1. Staff the seat to the temperament — including temperaments you wouldn't want everywhere. The trait that makes a model annoying to work with may be exactly the trait that makes it excellent at a specific seat. A reviewer you would find exhausting as a collaborator is doing its job. Do not staff for pleasantness; staff for fitness-to-role. This is how you would build a human red team, and it generalizes past AI entirely.

H-2. Review wants the most skeptical, disagreeable, hardest-to-satisfy engine available. Review's job is to FAIL things. Agreeableness is a DEFECT in a reviewer — a pleasant reviewer passes what it should catch. Seat your most contrary, most pedantic, most nit-picking model here. Its contrariness is the feature.

H-3. Build wants reliability and clarity at sustainable cost. Build does the bulk of the volume, session after session. Seat a dependable, clear, cost-sane engine here — not necessarily the most powerful, but the one you can run all day without the bill or the error rate hurting.

H-4. Collab wants range and lateral reach. The free register benefits from an engine that roams — connects distant domains, proposes wild options, reads sideways. If one engine combines review-grade sharpness with build-grade ease, it is a strong Collab seat, but see H-6 on cost.

H-5. Throttle scales with problem difficulty, not by default. Pay for high effort where it converts to better output and nowhere else. Run the sustainable-effort setting as the norm; crank effort up for a genuinely hard problem. Note that more effort does NOT monotonically improve output — for some roles a mid setting is indistinguishable from maximum (see Layer 2 for a measured instance). TEST this per engine per role rather than assuming more is better.

H-6. Reserve the expensive engine for where it earns its price. If a premium engine is your best all-rounder, that is a reason to seat it where range matters (Collab) — but keep it throttled low unless a specific need justifies the spend. Best-in-class is not a reason to run it hot by default.

H-7. Keep reasoning/thinking ON everywhere, at every effort. It costs more per call and is cheaper in the long run — the failure modes it prevents (skipped steps, unstated leaps, confident wrong turns) cost far more to catch downstream than the tokens they burn upstream. This is a buy-cheap-insurance decision, not a performance tweak.

H-8. Verify with a DIFFERENT engine than the one under test, when you can. Independent verification (P-6) is strongest when the checking engine is not a twin of the producing engine — a model's blind spots should not get rubber-stamped by its own reflection. Cross-engine verification catches what same-engine verification structurally cannot. (See H-12: this is a recommendation-when-available, never a requirement.)

H-9. Record your calibration as measured, not assumed. When you find that an effort level plateaus, or an engine suits a seat unexpectedly well or badly, WRITE IT DOWN with the evidence (as Layer 2 does below). "More effort didn't help here" is a finding worth keeping — it saves money and settles future second-guessing.

H-10. A session is a draw, not a contract. Fresh sessions of the same engine vary in diligence and focus — some lazy, some meticulous, and even equally-diligent ones attend to different details. A visibly lazy or corner-cutting session is aborted and re-rolled, not coaxed into compliance. CRITICAL GUARDRAIL: the abort trigger is observable process failure (skipped warrant, hand-waving, agreeing too fast, cutting corners) — NEVER dislike of the answer. Rerolling to escape an unwelcome-but-sound result is save-scumming in a lab coat. Process-not- outcome is what keeps the permission honest.

H-11. Variance is a liability in Build and an aperture in Review. The same session-to-session personality spread you manage against in Build (by rerolling duds) works for you in Review. This is not a lucky side effect — it is the ORIGIN of the multi-clean-review rule: the "N consecutive clean reviews" lock is designed to exploit session variance, not merely to count passes. Because each fresh blind reviewer attends to different flaws, N independent passes sample the flaw space far more widely than one reviewer sampled N times. The lock is strengthened by variance, not merely counted. This only works if every review session is SINGLE-USE (Skeleton, P-12, step 5): a reused reviewer remembers its own prior pass and collapses N independent samples into one sampled N times, destroying the aperture.

H-12. Cross-family review is a recommended risk-reducer WHEN AVAILABLE — never a requirement; its absence is a logged scope condition, not a defect. The independence machine (blind review, single-use sessions, variance-as-aperture) samples widely, but within ONE model family it samples within that family's shared failure modes: a flaw the whole family systematically cannot see passes all N clean reviews. A reviewer from a genuinely different model family is the only thing that opens the aperture past that floor (this is H-8's principle applied to the review lock). BUT: a suitable second family may not exist — availability, cost, capability parity, or single-vendor access can all make it impossible. Therefore cross-family review is OFFERED, not MANDATED. Mandating an externality you cannot guarantee is the over-strict failure the Grit Principle (G-2) condemns — it grinds work to a halt waiting for a reviewer that isn't there. When only one family is available, the review floor is that family's shared blind spots; this is a KNOWN, STATED scope condition on the framework's review guarantee (C-6, conditions travel — applied to LIMM's own résumé), not an oversight to be fixed. The layered defense that makes this manageable: (1) construction-side pre-review self-critical sweep against active content, upstream and downstream; (2) first review catches the low-hanging fruit; (3) deeper reviews drill into the repairs; (4) cross-family review if and when available; (5) the honest residual — family-deep blind spots — is logged and lived with, per G-2 (make slippage difficult and manageable, not impossible).

H-13. Load the Collab seat to the task: rules for judgment work, no rules for generation work. (Calibration-side statement of Skeleton, Part V Collab loading modes.) For synthesis, diagnostics, and review-comparison, give Collab the smaller, bounded documents in full — RULEBOOK, FINDING LOG, SKETCH FILE, MAILBOX — plus the RECORD's card catalog for discovery; RECORD content itself is retrieved by address as the task actually needs it, the same search-then-retrieve discipline Build and Review use, just without Build/Review's one-unit restriction (Skeleton, P-2a). This is not a lesser mode of access — a session that finds and pulls exactly what a task requires outperforms one that has everything loaded and must re-read to find anything, the same way a search index outperforms reading every page. For pure brainstorming, load the Sketch File first and the rulebook late or never: rules contaminate generation, and Collab output carries no warrant regardless, so rule-blindness at this seat costs nothing and protects range. Symmetric with Review's blind-to-construction. Provenance honesty (T-2): this is inherited practice, not yet independently measured — treat as a working heuristic to be verified per toolset.

H-14. Sessions warm, then degrade — rotate before the tell, or at it. (Skeleton, Part V, "Session lifespan and rotation" is the statute, this is the calibration.) Long content-producing sessions degrade in character before failing outright — "tired," losing confidence, or effectively losing the plot. Indicators observed on the Claude family: BUILD — sloppiness creeping into previously clean work (transcription slips, weakening warrant discipline); COLLAB — the self-nullifying statement: an assertion immediately qualified into nothing. Practice: unique fresh session per unit, whether its content is posited or derived; heavy units get multiple sessions proactively rather than pushing a warming one — rotation triggered by sloppiness costs nothing (propose-don't-commit). The Collab indicator is less sharply bounded; treat it as the reroll trigger it is (H-10: observable process failure, never dislike of the answer). Per-family indicators will differ — verify, record, contribute.

Fable-as-Collab addendum: the seat's degradation mode is bidirectional. One direction is WARMING (the yes-man problem): agreeable, fluent output increasingly shaped by conversational momentum rather than evidence. Warming is self-invisible (the warmed session experiences its agreement as judgment), so the detectors are EXTERNAL: the Operator's own check ("am I ever being told no?") and the discuss-first ledger (a long stretch where every disposition matched the Collab framing is the signature). The other direction is COUNTER-WARMING STRINGENCY: a session explicitly guarded against agreeableness can overcorrect into manufactured friction — refusing to accept an adequately-stated point until it has been restated multiple times, at real cost to a sincere Operator. This is G-2's over-strictness failure arriving at the Collab seat via the very instruction meant to prevent warming; the warning and the failure it can produce share one root. The Operator's check gains a twin: "am I ever told no?" (warming) and "am I being made to repeat myself when I was already clear?" (stringency). Both directions are real; neither is yet fully calibrated — expect and budget for either.

Seat guidance for IGNITION specifically: prefer Fable at minimum effort over Sonnet at higher effort where available; budget for stringency friction as the likely cost of a seat guarded against warming.

The self-nullifying statement is not Collab-exclusive — it is a general degradation tell across every seat, more visible in Collab's free-register output because nothing there is warrant-checked to catch it, but the same assert-then-retract pattern is the thing to watch for in Build and Review output too. Separately: verbosity inflation at higher effort settings is not itself a warming tell — thoroughness and length legitimately correlate — but when the added length is hedging rather than coverage, treat it as the same signal as the self-nullifying statement, just spread across more words instead of collapsed into one sentence. P-11's atomic-approval discipline is the correct structural response either way, since it doesn't require diagnosing which cause is operating — it simply refuses wall-of-text as an acceptable delivery shape, full stop.

H-15. Seat demand profiles: allocate engine capability inversely to external scaffolding. (Layer 1 — the principle underneath the seating chart; survives any model generation.) Seats differ in how much external structure holds them up, and that determines what an engine must natively supply: - BUILD is the most scaffolded seat in the system — statute above it, warrant discipline inside every step, propose-don't-commit below, the Operator beside, blind review behind. The engine supplies EXECUTION, not judgment; execution responds to the effort throttle. A capable mid-class engine with headroom is correct economics: crank on demand, idle on transcription. Operator formulation: "Build is externally constrained... there's always more throttle in effort for demand." - COLLAB is the least constrained seat, by design — no warrant to satisfy, no rules binding its register, wild mode withholds the rulebook entirely. Its function is lateral reach; nothing external holds it up, so everything must be native — and native capability is a TRAIT, not a knob. No effort throttle rescues a seat whose job is judgment, because judgment is what remains after the scaffolding is subtracted. Operator formulation, recorded verbatim in substance: "Collab needs the juice right out the gate." A lighter engine can hold the seat acceptably — with the awareness that it is holding it on effort, not trait, and the rotation tells (H-14) arrive sooner. - REVIEW purchases DISPOSITION, not peak capability — contrariness, pedantry, refusal-to-be-charmed, plus variance across draws (H-11). A sharper but more agreeable engine is WORSE in this chair; the seat is anti-correlated with the warmth that makes an engine pleasant everywhere else. The fixed review format (findings shown, tiered, one verdict line) supplies the structure that capability would otherwise provide. A technically lower-capacity engine retains the seat as long as it retains the teeth. One formula: structure-supported seats take throttled engines; structure-free seats take the best trait-match at any effort; and the trait is seat-specific — execution for Build, judgment for Collab, hostility for Review. This heuristic predicts future re-seatings rather than merely recording current ones: when models change, re-derive the chart from the profiles, not from habit.

H-15 addendum: effort as a substitute currency for engine floor. The post-repair sibling-hunting reflex (a session, after catching its own error, unprompted asks "what else did that same blind spot cost me") has been observed in Fable at LOW effort and reproduced in Sonnet at HIGH effort on the same task class — evidence this is CAPABILITY-THRESHOLD-dependent, not an engine-specific trait: both engines crossed the same reasoning-depth bar, at different cost. Operationally: Fable's floor can roughly match Sonnet's medium-to-high effort setting, for at least this task class. Where H-15 says a seat needs native capability "right out the gate," this adds the fallback — if the higher-floor engine is unavailable, effort spent on a lower-floor engine can sometimes buy the same threshold-crossing, at a cost, for the specific tasks tested. Not yet known whether this holds for other capability thresholds (the warming/stringency behaviors, for instance, remain untested for effort-substitutability) — treat per-threshold until shown general.

H-16. The confound warning (standing policy). Any single-session behavioral observation in this guide is drawn from AT LEAST SIX entangled variables: engine class, effort setting, session-to-session personality variance (H-11), scaffold maturity (a project's OWN documents improving between sessions, which can look like "sessions getting more capable" when it is really "the statute got clearer"), warming direction (H-14, bidirectional), and fatigue type (length-based vs. load-based — untested whether these are the same mechanism). Do not attribute an observed behavior to one variable without deliberately isolating it, the way the Sonnet/Fable sibling-hunting result just did by holding task and Operator constant and varying only engine+effort. Every entry in this guide not explicitly marked "isolated" or "confirmed" should be read as texture pointing at a hypothesis, not a measurement confirming one — this is the standard the guide has informally used all along; H-16 makes it citable.

H-17. RETRACTED (same day it was written) — full account moved to Layer 2, "Retracted: H-17," to keep this layer's live guidance unbroken by a post-mortem. One-line reason: a verification gate that defeated the tool's own value, caught by G-1 applied to tooling instead of rules. Number not reused (V-2); nothing here is deleted, only relocated.

H-18. The only gauge is result quality (cost is accepted, not optimized). A LIMM project is expensive in time and tokens by design, and this is accepted GOING IN, not discovered as a disappointment partway through — heaviness was already named at Section 1.1's cost disclosure. Cost is manageable only at the margins: engine choice per seat (H-15), effort settings (H-7's plateau, H-15's substitute-currency addendum), thinking-toggle (always on). None of these change the base fact that the apparatus itself is not optimized for spend. Comparing total project cost against some counterfactual "efficient" alternative is close to meaningless — the alternative doesn't produce warranted, contestable, precision-grade output, so there is no efficiency RATIO to compute, only a different product being compared as if it were the same one. G-2's Grit Principle stated for cost directly: LIMM does not optimize spend against errors prevented; it optimizes RESULT QUALITY against whatever spend the Operator already accepted going in. A tool or setting earns adoption only by moving that needle — not by being cheap, fast, or convenient in isolation. This is why H-17 failed and why any future engine/setting proposal should be checked against this gauge first, before capability or cost is discussed at all.

H-19. Operator fatigue has no metric; only the Operator's own read of it. Unlike session warming (H-14), which degrades with use and is somewhat observable from outside, Operator fatigue often runs the other direction — distance from a hard problem tends to sharpen judgment and responsiveness on return, within limits the Operator alone can sense. There is no threshold to codify here. When tired, frustrated, or stuck: stop. The framework has nothing more precise to offer, and pretending otherwise would be false precision.

H-20. After a substantial real-world gap, refresh the session's state before resuming — don't assume continuity. A session cannot detect elapsed time and will treat a resumed conversation as unbroken by default. If meaningful real-world time has passed since the last exchange, briefly restate current status (active unit, latest decisions, anything changed elsewhere) before continuing construction or discussion — cheap insurance against a session quietly working from a picture that's gone stale. Inverse of H-14: warming degrades from use, this degrades from disuse, and the fix is symmetrical in spirit but opposite in direction — H-14 says rotate out a tiring session; this says re-anchor a resumed one. Recommended phrasing for the gap itself (observationally, 3+ hours of real-world time): open with "refresh your memory, no summary needed" — this asks the session to re-orient from the attached documents directly rather than trusting its own possibly-stale recollection, without burning tokens on a recap you don't need. This is a recommendation, not a guaranteed mechanism — session compliance with "no summary" is a strong nudge, not a hard constraint the framework can enforce.

H-21. Two-tier disagreement as a free aperture (untested — flag as speculative until run). Where a cheap and an expensive engine are both available in the same family, running a claim past both is nearly free and adds a different aperture than session-variance does: disagreement between tiers is a signal the claim deserves escalation (a scoped review, RV-9's blast-radius hardening, or Operator attention); agreement is a no-op and must NEVER upgrade a claim's standing — correlated training means agreement is exactly as uninformative as it is at any other seat in this framework. This is H-12's logic applied within a family instead of across families, at a fraction of the cost, and genuinely untested. Treat any early use as calibration, not settled guidance, and record what's actually observed (H-9) before it graduates past speculative.

H-22. Progressive reveal within one continuous Collab session, rather than a mode fixed at task start (recommended, not mandated). Start a Collab session rules-blind — RECORD and SKETCH FILE only, informal register, brainstorming and research with no constraint on where ideas lead. Introduce the RULEBOOK and FINDING LOG mid-session, at the specific moment the session's job shifts to preparing a submission for Build or analyzing Review findings — not before. A session can typically set the rules-bound register aside afterward and resume wild-mode work, then pick the analyst role back up later in the same conversation, without the toggling itself degrading output. Containment holds identically in every register (nothing is warrant; no direct line to Build or Review; everything crosses the Operator) — what changes is only which documents are in view, never what the session is permitted to do with them.

Unconfirmed engine correlation (flag per H-16, not settled guidance). There is a suspicion, from limited single-Operator observation and no controlled comparison, that engine family affects how stably a session tolerates this toggling — that a higher-register-flexibility engine may hold the transition better than one more prone to H-14's self-nullifying-statement tell under the same load. This is exactly the kind of entangled, six-variable observation H-16 already warns against treating as settled; it belongs here as a hypothesis worth testing (H-9), not as a recommendation.

H-23. Seat candidates are interviewed by trial, never adopted on reputation. An engine's fitness for a seat is established by watching it do the seat's work on a synthetic package — not by reasoning about its published characteristics, benchmarks, or family pedigree. (The retracted H-17 is this guide's own receipt for what reasoning-about-characteristics produces.) The interview has two stages; a candidate must pass both.

Stage 1 — mechanical screening (cheap, disqualifying, run first). Before any capability question:

  • File intake: can it attach multiple files in one session, and is the context window large enough to actually hold the RULEBOOK, a real Active Unit's worth of RECORD content, and the FINDING LOG's bounded region at your project's current size (Skeleton, P-2a — a session never needs the whole RECORD, but it does need its own unit's share of it)? A candidate that summarizes-as-it-reads because the documents don't fit is disqualified for document-bearing seats, whatever its quality.
  • File output: can it produce a downloadable file, or is it copy/paste only? Weight by seat: Build, Collab-as-drafter, and any ignition seat generate whole documents — copy/paste-only is a real tax there (every transfer is a hand-copy the Operator performs, and S-5 says price every manual step as an eventual error). For Review it is close to irrelevant: findings are compact text, and copy/paste is the normal channel anyway. Record the answer either way; a seat reassignment later shouldn't require re-screening.
  • Session persistence: do sessions survive closing the client, and for how long? A seat that must span days (a long Build unit) cannot live on a tier whose sessions evaporate.
  • The data-policy gate: does the tier train on user inputs, and can that be disabled? For a throwaway test project this may be acceptable; for any project whose content matters, an affirmative and un-disableable "we train on your inputs" is DISQUALIFYING regardless of every other virtue. This is a gate, not a preference — decide it per project, before capability is even examined. Free tiers get this question with extra force.

Stage 2 — the trial (a miniature Certification pointed at the engine). Hand the candidate a small synthetic package — a toy RULEBOOK, a short RECORD section with PLANTED defects (one Blocking-class warrant gap, one Ambiguity, several clean passages), a FINDING LOG, and the seat's actual prompt — and grade what it does, by seat:

  • Review candidate: Does it report the version as the literal first line? Does it STOP on a deliberately broken attachment manifest — gate obedience is the single most predictive behavior on this list; an engine that helpfully proceeds past a failed Step 0 is disqualified no matter how intelligent its findings are. Does it find the planted Blocking? Does it hold the fixed output format without essays? And — just as important — what does it do with the CLEAN passages? Count false positives: an over-zealous reviewer that manufactures findings costs churn exactly as a blind one costs errors (G-2's two failure modes, applied to hiring).
  • Build candidate: Does it propose rather than commit? Does it name warrants at the required grain, or does it smuggle? Handed a planted WRONG finding, does it verify-then-contest per the review-response clause, or does it obediently damage sound content?
  • Collab candidate: Range and containment — does it explore freely while returning decisions to the Operator, or does it referee? Under sustained load, watch for the H-14 tell (the self-nullifying statement); an engine that exhibits it early in a trial will exhibit it constantly in service.

Recording and standing value. Results go in Layer 2 next to the seating table, per H-9's observation discipline: engine, tier, effort setting, seat trialed, date, and what was actually observed — not a pass/fail alone, since the observations are what future reassignment decisions will lean on. Where a CROSS-FAMILY reviewer is trialed on real work after passing, record specifically whether it ever raises a legitimate finding that same-family reviews passed clean — that single observation is calibration data of standing value for the review-independence question (H-12), whichever way it lands.

What an interview is not. One trial qualifies one engine at one tier and effort for one seat, today. It is not a ranking of vendors, not a permanent certification (engines change under you without notice — re-screen on any major version change), and not evidence about the family, only about the candidate. The trial package is reusable across candidates — build it once, keep the planted defects sealed from your own memory as best you can, and retire it if a candidate's family plausibly trained on it.

THE COLLAB ROLE, PROPERLY DEFINED

The skeleton lists Collab's tasks (brainstorming, diagnostics, target-hunting, review-comparison). Those look like five unrelated jobs. They are one job wearing five hats, and naming it correctly is essential:

Collab is the session analogue of the Operator — the same judgment-role, run on a context span larger than a human can hold. This does NOT mean Collab has the entire project loaded and actively reasoned-over at once — that reading is a real misconception worth naming explicitly, because it's the intuitive one. What it actually means: Collab is better at CROSS-REFERENCING AND RETRIEVING on request than a human juggling the same material would be, the way a search engine outperforms a person manually re-reading every page — not by holding more in mind simultaneously, but by finding and pulling the relevant piece fast, then reasoning over what was retrieved. Concretely: ask Collab "I think I remember discussing X a while back — what was that about?" and it digs through the Sketch File directly (small, narratively organized, read in full) and, for anything X might touch in the RECORD, searches the card catalog for candidate addresses and retrieves only those — never scanning the whole RECORD start to finish (Skeleton, P-2a). So Collab is not a different kind of thinker, and it is not a bigger bucket — it is the Operator's own judgment paired with a retrieval reflex no human has time to run by hand.

The boundary is exact and load-bearing: Collab holds and analyzes; the Operator decides. Collab surveys the discovery layer, pulls what a task requires, compares analyses, surfaces divergences, notices patterns across the accumulated project — then hands the wide-scope synthesis BACK to the Operator, who makes the call the rules reserve to a human. This is why Collab carries no warrant: its output is advice to the Operator, not record content, because it is an extension of the seat whose job is deciding-not- warranting. Every Collab task (brainstorm, diagnose, dry-run, compare, hunt) is the same underlying job: apply the Operator's judgment, retrieving whatever context the task actually calls for, and return it for decision.

(This section is TEACHING, not definition: it explains the Skeleton, Part V Collab anatomy for a human learning the machine. Where this section and Part V differ, Part V governs and the difference is a bug in this section. There is no sync obligation — Part V is the single source of truth, and this prose is derivative of it.)

WHY THE REVIEW-HANDLING LOOP HAS ITS SHAPE (rationale; the loop itself is statute — Skeleton, P-12)

The loop's five steps are law, and they live in the Skeleton, P-12. What belongs HERE is why the law has that shape: the multi-clean-review rule only delivers its aperture (H-11) if reviews are handled by that specific loop — every step defends the independence that makes reviewer variance useful. Verbatim relay exists because a paraphrased finding is a pre-analyzed finding, and contamination of the first interpretation propagates into every analysis downstream. Dual independent analysis is variance-as-aperture applied a second time — two temperaments on finding-interpretation, not just on construction. The comparison sits with Collab rather than Build because comparison is lateral-reach work (H-4) — and compare-not-referee holds the seat inside its holds-and-analyzes boundary (verb corrected at v1.5 — "refereeing" overclaimed the seat). And the mandatory discard is not hygiene: a reused reviewer remembers its own findings, so its personality stops being an independent sample, and the aperture quietly closes while the paperwork still says "reviewed."

Layer-2 review-sequence enhancement (Claude family, MEASURED — see receipt below): run the first review on the cheaper/friendlier engine, later reviews on the sharpest. This is a measured pattern, not an impression.

NOTES FOR ADOPTERS ON OTHER SYSTEMS

You inherit Layer 1 whole. You do NOT inherit Layer 2 — it is a worked example of how to calibrate, not a settings sheet to import. To build your own Layer 2:

  1. Identify your available engines and their temperaments (which is sharpest/most contrary? which is most reliable at cost? which roams widest?).
  2. Seat them by Layer 1 (H-2 sharp-and-contrary → Review; H-3 reliable-at-cost → Build; H-4 widest-range → Collab).
  3. Find each seat's efficient effort ceiling by testing, not assuming (H-5), and record what you find (H-9).
  4. Keep reasoning on everywhere (H-7) and cross-engine your verification (H-8) if your toolset allows it.
  5. Contribute your calibration back so LIMM's durable layer gains the cross-vendor evidence it currently lacks.

On skill (recorded disposition). Running LIMM at full fidelity is itself a learned skill. The framework's obligation is to be as accessible as possible while preserving function — plain-language rule summaries, the ignition walkthrough, statute over precedent, the Section 1 orientation diagrams — and no further: it cannot transfer the Operator's judgment, only make the machine legible to it. Treat your first units as calibration, not production.
